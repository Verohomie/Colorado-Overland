📜 Mining History — Animas Forks

⛏️ The ghost town at the forks
Animas Forks grew up in 1875 where the forks of the Animas River meet — first called "Three Forks of the Animas." Speculative silver money pushed it to nearly 500 residents by the early 1880s, with general stores, boarding houses, a jail, the grand Kalamazoo House hotel, and the Animas Forks Pioneer (1882–1886), which billed itself as the highest newspaper in the United States. A fire in 1891 and the 1893 Silver Crash gutted it — it's been a ghost town since the 1920s. The restored two-story Duncan House (William Duncan, 1879), with the bay window facing the valley, is the iconic shot. Walk through with the BLM interpretive signs; don't enter unstable structures. 📷 Photos to grab: Wikimedia: Animas Forks · Denver Public Library (historic B&W) · History Colorado

📜 Mining History — Engineer Pass, Henson & the Ute-Ulay

⛏️ Otto Mears' toll road & the mine that built Lake City
Engineer Pass Road opened in the late 1870s as a major stagecoach and freight route. Like so many roads here it was a toll road built by Otto Mears — the Estonian-born "Pathfinder of the San Juans," who finished the Lake City–Ouray wagon road in 1877. Watch for the interpretive kiosks along the route that describe key features.

As you drop the Henson Creek side toward Lake City, take time to see the old town of Henson and the Ute-Ulay Mine. Prospectors Harry Henson, Joel Mullen, Albert Meade & Charles Goodwin struck the Ute and Ulay veins on August 27, 1871 — the area's first big silver-lead discovery. It went into production in 1874, sold to the Crooke Mining & Smelting Co. for $125,000 in 1876, and the wealth it threw off is what sparked the development of Lake City (where Enos Hotchkiss's Golden Fleece was another rich producer). The 1893 Silver Crash ended the boom; near the pass, the camp of Mineral Point faded with it. 📷 Photos to grab: Western Mining History: Otto Mears toll road · Legends of America: Henson & Ute-Ulay · History Colorado: Ute-Ulay · Wikimedia: Lake City

📜 Mining History — Red Mountain District

⛏️ The richest few square miles on the route
Today's Million Dollar Highway threads the Red Mountain Mining District — the most prolific in the area. John Robinson struck the fabulously rich Yankee Girl and Guston silver mines in 1881; before 1900 the Yankee Girl was reckoned one of the most valuable mines in the country. Otto Mears (again) built the toll road — finished 1883, the ancestor of today's highway — and then the Silverton Railroad (1887–89) to haul ore down to Ironton and Albany. The district gave up over $30 million in silver, lead, zinc, copper and gold before the 1893 Silver Crash; the consolidated Idarado Mine later reworked the old claims from the 1930s until 1978. Stop at the pullouts near the top of Red Mountain Pass — the rust-red waste dumps and the leaning Yankee Girl headframe are the classic photos. 📷 Photos to grab: Wikimedia: Red Mountain District · Wikimedia: Yankee Girl Mine · Legends of America: Red Mountain · Denver Public Library (historic)

🔧 Land Cruiser / Trip Notes

  • Air-down kit + compressor — you'll use it on Medano sand, the Alpine Loop, and the Rimrocker.
  • Carry spare fuel + 2 days of water before the Alpine Loop and the Rimrocker — services are sparse.
  • Winter-rated sleeping bags: three nights sleep above ~10,000 ft and can hit freezing in late June.
  • Rain jacket, gloves, hat for Handies — summit is 15–25°F colder than camp, off the top by noon.
  • Download offline OnX/Gaia maps for the whole route in Durango (Day 3) while on wifi.
  • Check fire bans (BLM Moab, Rio Grande NF, San Juan NF) — common by late June.

🖼️ Photos — How to Populate the Guide

The guide expects images named images/00.jpg (cover), images/01.jpg … 09.jpg (day banners), and images/day1-map.png … day9-map.png (route maps). Until they exist, each shows a labeled placeholder.

• Run python3 download_images.py to auto-fetch free, public-domain landmark photos from Wikimedia Commons into /images.
• Or drop your own photos into /images with those exact names.
• IMAGES.md lists every photo slot with 2–3 source links (Wikimedia, NPS, BLM, Forest Service) you can scrape from — all free to use.
• For route maps, screenshot each day's Google Maps directions (or OnX) and save as dayN-map.png.

⚠️ Plan, don't gospel. Dispersed sites, road status, and weather change fast in the San Juans and the desert. Verify camps and creek/pass conditions in OnX/Gaia and with the ranger numbers above the day before. Build in flex — Day 8 is your buffer.
